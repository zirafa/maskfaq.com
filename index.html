How well do homemade mask materials compare against the N95 standard?

Some materials make better masks than others. To better understand makeshift mask filter performance, samples of commonly used homemade mask materials were sent to TSI, Inc who tested them using the same machine and test procedure used to give N95 masks their NIOSH certification.[1]

Mask Fit & Handling

Regardless of material used, keep in mind that proper mask fit and proper donning/doffing procedure must be followed to achieve maximum respiratory protection and to avoid contamination. A leaky or dirty mask cannot be relied upon.

Test Results

The table below shows the results of different mask materials tested by TSI, Inc using a TSI 8130a test machine. This filtration testing instrument is used by NIOSH to certify N95 respirators (42 CFR Part 84) and is also used by respirator manufacturers to test their product. These tests use a particle size distribution specified by the NIOSH standard. A material with a higher % efficiency will filter a greater amount of small particle aerosols and large droplets (only non-oil aerosols). A higher resistance (pressure drop) means a material is less breathable and will be uncomfortable to wear. The ideal mask has a high efficiency rating with a low resistance. The material used in most N95 masks uses a special electrostatic media to achieve a high filter efficiency while retaining breathability. It is unlikely any DIY mask material can truly achieve the N95 filter rating while remaining breathable, but the only way to know is to test them!

  • Material: material(s) tested
  • Flow L/min: volumetric flow rate. Set to ~60 L/min for a standard filter holder area of 100 cm^2.
  • Resistance (mm H20): Resistance (pressure drop). A higher number means it will be harder to breathe. A typical N95 mask has a pressure drop around 9 mm H20.
  • % Penetration: percentage of test particles that passed through
  • % Efficiency: percentage of test particles that could not pass through. An N95 mask has a minimum efficiency rating of 95%.

Testing notes

N95 respirators[1] are tested at 85 L/min and a typical respirator is ~160 cm^2. A salt aerosol (NaCl) is used as the test agent. Although salt particles are .1Âµm, the test results are based on a distribution of particle sizes specified by the NIOSH standard.

These tests were performed using a TSI 8130a tester with salt aerosol as the test agent and a standard filter holder area of 100 cm^2. Because of the difference in area the flow rate was adjusted to ~60 L/min so the velocity through the samples was approximately the same as on a mask. For some of the smaller samples an adapter was used that lowered the test area and the flow rate was adjusted accordingly. With these adjustments in flow the pressure drop (resistance) should be similar to how the fabric would work as a mask and the % Penetration (or % Efficiency) should be approximately the same.

For another project TSI tested 20 N95 masks and the average penetration was about 1.8% (Efficiency 98.2%) and typical pressure drop was about 9 mm H2O. The limit on pressure drop for respirators is 35 mm H2O.
